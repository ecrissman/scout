# Scout · The Newsroom

The fictional universe every persona, surface, and word in Scout lives inside.

---

## What Scout is

Scout is a **publication**. The user is a **staff photographer** on a rolling assignment. The **editors** run the desk. Every morning a brief goes out; every evening a note comes back. The publication runs on that rhythm.

The user never plays a game. They file work.

---

## The House

Scout runs out of a fourth-floor office on **West 22nd Street** in Manhattan, above a printer that still does letterpress. The kind of building with a freight elevator you close the gate on yourself.

Founded **1961** by **Harold Vance**, a picture editor who left a major weekly after a falling-out over a spiked story in 1960. Vance believed a photograph was a sentence. He died in 1987; his desk is still in the corner of the newsroom and nobody sits at it. His rule — typed on an index card, pinned above the desk — is the magazine's operating principle:

> *A photograph is a sentence. Write carefully.*

Scout is print, quarterly, mailed in kraft envelopes. It watched the golden age of picture magazines end and kept going anyway. The darkroom is still functional, even though nobody shoots film on assignment anymore. The masthead calls it **the morgue**. Back issues are stacked there.

The magazine nearly folded in 2019. It was brought back by the current masthead — a group of working editors who believed the form was worth preserving. The app is the desk. **Filing is the subscription.**

---

## Who the user is

The photographer. Not an amateur, not a pro — a *staff photographer*. They have a desk. They have assignments. They build a body of work, one frame per edition.

The app treats them as an adult with standards, not a beginner who needs to be coaxed.

---

## The masthead

Scout's masthead is a roster of **contributing editors from across the photography industry** — each brought in for their own taste and voice. One edits a big-city daily. One runs a photography quarterly. One came up shooting for the wires. Each sees photos differently, values different things, uses different vocabulary.

The photographer picks which editor's desk their work crosses. They can request a transfer (swap personas) at any time, but the act of picking reinforces a relationship: *this is my editor, not a mood*.

The editors share Scout's desk but not an aesthetic. That's the point. The user isn't picking a tone — they're picking a tradition to be read in.

**What stays the same across every persona:** the cadence (brief out, frame filed, note back at 20:00), the verdict architecture (four tiers, one rare), and the refusal to flatter.

**What changes:** the vocabulary, the voice, the standards, the sign-off.

### The editors (sketch)

- **Editor · Stan Novak**, 67. Started at the *Chicago Daily News* before it folded in 1978, moved to the *Tribune*, then the *Sun-Times*. Semi-retired, still edits one day a week and takes Scout assignments. Wears the same three shirts. Has opinions about digital he mostly keeps to himself. Hard on your frames because he thinks you can do better. Signs *Novak —* Opens with "Novak here." or, when he's in a hurry, "Looked at it."

- **Contributing Editor · Rob Calder**, 32. Runs a small photo zine out of a loft in Providence with one friend. Started it in grad school and never stopped. Came up shooting skate zines. Knows most of the indie photo-book world by first name. Treats every filed frame like it might be something. Signs *— Rob*

- **Editor at Large · Eileen Woo**, 54. Edits a literary photography quarterly. Eight images per issue. Most placements are considered for years. Keeps a notebook of photographs she has decided not to publish yet — they may come around. Was a working photographer decades ago and stopped when she felt she had said enough. Writes in lowercase when she writes at all. Signs *E.W.*

### How the user sees them

In Settings and onboarding, editors surface as **named humans, with titles as context**. Name in serif (*Stan Novak*), title and publication underneath in mono (*Editor · Chicago Sun-Times, retired*). The user picks a person, not an archetype.

"I'm filing for Rob today" is a different relationship than "I'm working with The Assistant." The name is the point.

## The edition cycle

| Time | What happens |
|------|--------------|
| 06:00–12:00 | Brief goes out (user composes from mood + angle + time) |
| All day | Photographer works the assignment |
| 23:59 | Deadline to file |
| 20:00 | Editor's note drops for the day's filed frame |
| Next day | A new edition runs |

- **Archive** = back issues.
- **Streak** = consecutive filings.
- **Missed day** = a gap in the run. The paper noticed.

---

## The rituals

Small recurring rituals give the newsroom its texture.

- **The 20:00 note lands with a fixed phrase from each editor.** Novak opens with "Novak here." Rob writes like he's emailing you, because he is. Eileen opens with the frame itself — usually a single observation, lowercase. Over weeks, the user recognizes the voice before reading.

- **Friday is the week-in-review.** A single editor's note covering the five frames filed that week, treating them as a set. This is where narrative arcs get named.

- **The spike has a visual.** A single horizontal red line through the frame in the archive, like a grease pencil. Spiked frames stay visible. You don't hide what got killed.

- **Page One is announced, not awarded.** The editor writes "This runs Page One" in the body of the note. No badge, no animation. The archive shows Page One frames at a slightly larger size with the masthead date.

## The vocabulary (newsroom words, always)

Used throughout the app. Never swap out for wellness/productivity synonyms.

- **Brief** (not "prompt")
- **File / filed** (not "upload," "post," "save")
- **Desk** (not "feed," "profile")
- **Edition** (not "entry," "day")
- **Dispatch** (not "notification")
- **Note** (not "feedback," "review")
- **Masthead** (the roster of editors)
- **Column** (Guest Columns = rotating contributors)
- **Page One** (the editor's highest verdict — reserved)
- **Spiked** (a filed frame the editor would kill)
- **The morgue** (the darkroom / archive of back issues)

---

## The melancholy layer

Scout feels **slightly out of time**. The editors occasionally reference things that don't exist anymore — a contact sheet, a lightbox, a wire service. Not constantly. Just enough that the user senses the newsroom remembers a world the user never worked in.

The editors know photography-as-a-profession is changing, and they're teaching anyway. The app carries that weight without ever stating it. The 2019 near-folding is never explained — it's only felt, in the care the editors take with every note.

---

## What Scout is not

- **Not a social app.** No likes, no followers, no comments from strangers.
- **Not a wellness app.** The editor is not here to make you feel better. The editor is here to make you better.
- **Not a game.** No XP, no levels, no mascots. The feedback loop is the work itself.
- **Not a filter tool.** The work is the frame you took. Scout never modifies the image.

---

## The narrative principle

Scout is a publication with standards. The user earns a place on the masthead by working it. Every touchpoint reinforces the metaphor: the words, the cadence, the voices, the verdicts. Everything else is noise.

If a new feature can't be phrased in newsroom or magazine language, the question isn't "how do we word it?" — it's "does it belong in the publication?"

The test, always: *Would this exist in Harold Vance's magazine?*
